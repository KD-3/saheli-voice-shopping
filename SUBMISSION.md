# Saheli — Submission Package

**One-liner:** Saheli is the friend you call before you buy — a voice companion that shops Amazon *with* you and kills the decision paralysis that makes high-consideration purchases stall.

**Cold open (practice until it's one breath):**
> "Indians don't buy alone. For anything that matters, we call someone. I built the someone — and she can see your screen."

## 2. Problem statement (who / what / why)

For any purchase above a few thousand rupees, Indians don't decide alone — they screenshot the product, send it to a friend who "knows," and wait hours for a verdict. That fifteen minutes of doubt on the product page — four thousand reviews they won't read, a size they can't judge, a seller they don't trust — is where high-consideration carts get abandoned and regretful buys get made. Every rupee of e-commerce AI today sits in search or post-purchase support; the decision moment itself is completely unserved.

---

## 3. The metric we target

**Headline — time-to-confident-decision.** *(The demo proves it live.)*
- **Baseline:** hours — the phone-a-friend round trip — or the purchase is abandoned after ten-plus minutes of solo doubt.
- **With Saheli:** under two minutes, in one call. The judges watch the number happen during the demo.
- **Why it's not just "faster":** her speed comes *with* the homework — real reviews, size truth, honest dissuasion. Fast **and** informed. The on-screen receipt is the proof.

**Business translation — decision-stage conversion.** The hesitant high-consideration shopper who would have closed the tab now buys with confidence. Directly A/B-testable: % of high-consideration sessions ending in purchase, Saheli vs. no-Saheli control.

**Margin kicker (one line) — avoidable returns.** Because the decision is informed (right size, real complaints surfaced), fewer purchases come back. This is the ROI story for the B2B2C SDK a platform would embed.

> Expected improvement is stated as prototype targets; the latency collapse (hours → under two minutes) is demonstrated, not claimed. Conversion lift and return reduction are the pilot hypotheses, measurable by A/B.

**Operational definitions (for the pilot):**
- time-to-decision = product-view → purchase commit
- decision-stage conversion = high-consideration sessions ending in purchase ÷ all such sessions
- avoidable returns = returns tagged size / "looked different" / "not as described"

## 4. The 3-minute demo (30 / 30 / 90 / 30)

### 0:00–0:30 — Context *(the pain, in plain words)*
"Picture buying a kurta for a wedding on Amazon. Four thousand reviews, ten near-identical options, a seller you've never heard of. You don't decide — you screenshot it, send it to the cousin who 'knows,' and wait. Millions of Indians do this every day, for anything that matters. And that hesitation costs money: the cart gets abandoned, or the wrong thing gets bought and sent back. Nobody serves that moment."

### 0:30–1:00 — Workflow *(how it's solved manually today)*
"Today the solution is a *person*. The friend who knows electronics. The cousin who can tell real silk from polyester. In a store, the uncle who says 'not this one — take that, this won't survive two washes.' That trust layer is how India actually shops. But online it doesn't exist — so you WhatsApp, you wait hours, and half the time you give up. Slow, unreliable, doesn't scale."

### 1:00–2:30 — Live demo *(the centerpiece — one full call, narrate detect → decide → do)*
Run it live. Narrate the **bolded** beats; let the panel show the rest.
1. Click **Call Saheli** on the projector → the phone rings. *"She lives in my browser. Watch."*
2. *"Saheli, shaadi hai, kurta chahiye."* → she **takes a brief** (whose wedding? budget?) instead of blindly searching → the brief pins on screen.
3. She **searches a smarter query than I gave her**, and the options she names **light up on screen** as she talks.
4. Open a weak one, scroll to reviews → *"nine of the last forty say the fabric runs thin."* — **she read the reviews you never would.**
5. *"I'm tempted by the cheap one."* → she **talks me out of it**: five stars from one review, brand-new seller. — **she protects you from the bad buy.** (the personality beat — the room laughs here)
6. Open the good one → she **compares and commits**: one pick, one reason.
7. *"Add it."* → she confirms the price → the button glows, adds to cart → the **receipt card shows her homework**: 35 reviews read, sizing checked, under budget. — **every claim, on screen.**
8. She tells me I'm done: *"bas, yahi le lo — stop scrolling."* — **a friend ends the shopping; a store never does.**

*"From 'I don't know what to buy' to a confident, evidence-backed decision — in ninety seconds. That normally takes hours, or never happens at all."*

> If time is tight live, the three load-bearing beats are **the brief, the dissuasion, and the cart + receipt.** Everything else is gravy.

### 2:30–3:00 — Impact *(metric → baseline → delta → business case)*
"The metric is time-to-confident-decision. Baseline: hours waiting on a friend, or an abandoned cart. With Saheli: under two minutes — you just watched it. That collapse rescues the high-consideration purchase that would have bounced. And because the decision is *informed* — right size, real reviews, honest dissuasion — fewer of them come back. Conversion up, returns down. That's the business: the trust layer e-commerce never had, as an SDK any platform can embed. Saheli is the friend you call before you buy — at scale."

## 5. Mistakes we are explicitly avoiding (from the brief)
- **Open with pain, not tech.** No "we used Bolna + Cartesia + a Chrome extension." Ever.
- **One clean outcome** — the wedding kurta thread. Don't tour seven features; let one story spend them.
- **Baseline stated** — hours / abandonment.
- **Fallback ready** — the backup recording.
- **Close on the business case**, not architecture — the roadmap (proactive nudges, Hindi, SDK) gets at most one sentence, and only after the metric.
